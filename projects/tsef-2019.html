Rocket Payload Separation

Oct. 2018 - Mar. 2019

Overview

This is a blog of my Tidewater Science and Engineering Fair project, which I completed with a partner during my junior year of high school.


In this project titled Rocket Payload Separation to Achieve a Target Apogee, we designed a mechanical method of separation a rocket payload.


Technologies we used in this project include the Mojo 3D-printer, Arduino Nano, RockSim, and Fritzing.


In February of 2019, we presented this project at the Ocean Lakes Night of Science where we were awarded the MSA Research Medallion. Later, in March of 2019, we competed at the Tidewater Science and Engineering Fair and won a 1st place team special award from the Armed Forces Communications & Electronics Association (AFCEA), Tidewater chapter.

Introduction

Identifying a Problem

One must account for many factors such as wind, drag force, and weight of the rocket in order to design a rocket that will achieve the desired altitude (Benson, 2014). However, the constantly changing conditions often lead to errors in predictions.


To achieve a target apogee, we will develop a program that will autonomously control a mechanism to separate the payload. The separation will increase the surface area, thus introducing more drag (Hall, 2015). This will reduce upward motion and allow the rocket to reach the desired altitude. Additionally, this system will allow a rocket to reach different target altitudes without the need to redesign a new one. This project will test an innovative approach for reaching a target apogee.

Research Question and Hypothesis

Can the payload of a model rocket be separated early to achieve an apogee closer to the target altitude?


If the rocketâ€™s payload separates prior to reaching the projected altitude, then the separation will introduce more drag which will slow down the rocket so that it will reach an apogee closer to the target altitude.

Engineering Goal

Create a program and design and build a mechanism that will consistently separate the payload from the rocket autonomously at a predetermined altitude.

Risks and Safety

Due to the use of rocket motors and igniters, contingency plans have been developed and safety precautions for each of these have been noted, as listed in the outlined material safety data sheet.


The Estes Model Rocket Engine must be stored in a cool, dry location to avoid possible combustion. It must be protected against water and humidity. Any sources of ignition must be kept away. When launching the model rocket, a deflection plate must be used due to fire and projection hazards. In the event of a fire, it will be extinguished using normal procedures.


The Estes Model Rocket Igniter must be stored in a cool, dry location to avoid possible combustion. Any impact, friction, sparks, open flame, and high temperatures must be avoided as well. In the event of a fire, it will be extinguished using water, following normal procedures.


All National Association of Rocketry (NAR) safety guidelines must be followed.

Conclusion

Challenges and Lessons Learned

Our initial plan was to use a BBC Micro:bit as a circuit board, but we found that in order to use the Micro:bit, many libraries would have to be imported to connect to the sensor and allow the code to work. Despite its small size, it was also not small enough to fit inside the electronics container. Due to these complications, we decided to use an Arduino Nano.


We also encountered issues with connecting the servos and altimeter to the Arduino Nano. Since we did not have a lot of prior experience with electronics, our project supervisor provided us with materials and assistance in figuring out where to place connections.


All the electronics and parts had to fit within a 56mm diameter area since the top section of the rocket housed the electronics. The servo holder and electronics compartment were designed to be very compact.


The center rack piece had to be held steady in order to keep separation consistent. In order to do this, we had to reprint the servo holder and rack piece with grooves for better alignment.


Since our rocket did not use a traditional design, in which the shock cord is attached to the nose cone and the motor mount, we had to improvise by drilling holes into the electronics container and the parachute container, and the shock cord was connected to those. The electronics container was held inside the rocket by tying shock cord to the holes that were drilled and a hole drilled into the upper end of the rocket. A piece from another model rocket kit was then used to cover the hole in the rocket and keep the rocket relatively aerodynamic.

Results

We initially anticipated that our mechanism would cause consistent separation. We also expected that it would allow us to reach an altitude closer to the target apogee. The whole mechanism did work fairly reliably on the ground when the parachute was not inside the rocket.


However, once the parachute was placed inside the rocket, the mechanism stuck to a cord, and this lack of motion as the servos turned caused a gear to break off. Our conclusion is that by increasing the size of the top section of the rocket and redesigning the electronics container and the parachute container, the mechanism would work successfully and more consistently as a result of the increased space to work in and better storage for cords and parachute shroud lines.


Once redesigned, this method of separation will provide rocketeers with another reliable way of reaching target apogees.
